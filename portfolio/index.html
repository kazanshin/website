What I keep coming back to

Better learning should be easier to repeat.

Schools do not usually fail because nobody cares. They fail because care does not automatically become structure.

Good intentions need systems. Teachers need tools they can actually use. Students need routines that help them think, not just comply. Leaders need ways to make judgment more consistent without turning schools into machines. That's the work I keep coming back to: building practical systems that make better learning easier to notice, support, and sustain. I'm interested in the space between theory and Monday morning, where research, school leadership, teacher development, curriculum, communication, and operational reality all collide.

That's where the work gets difficult. That's also where it gets interesting.

Approach

Clear systems. Humane implementation. Better learning.

Start with the learning problem.

Strong educational design begins by identifying what students need to understand, practice, notice, or become able to do, then building systems around that target.

Make teacher support practical.

Teachers need feedback and tools they can actually use under real classroom conditions, not abstract ideals that collapse during a busy school day.

Design for sustainability.

School improvement should make good practice easier to repeat, easier to explain, and easier to sustain across people, classes, and time.
